LU Decomposition¶

import numpy as np
import scipy.linalg as la
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

Any matrix \(A\) has decomposition of the form \(A = PLU\) where

  • \(P\) is a permutation matrix

  • \(L\) is unit lower triangular

  • \(U\) is upper triangular

This is called the LU decomposition with partial pivoting.

Example 1¶

Find a decomposition \(A = LU\) for

\[\begin{split} A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 2 \\ 4 & 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{split}\]

using Gaussian elimination without pivoting and compare to the LU decomposition with partial pivoting computed by scipy.linalg.lu.

First, let us comute by hand:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} \phantom{-}1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 2 \\ 4 & 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} &= \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & \phantom{+}1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{array} \right] \\ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & \phantom{+}1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{array} \right] &= \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & \phantom{+}1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{array} \right] \end{align*} \end{split}\]

Therefore \(A = LU\) with

\[\begin{split} L = \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & -1 & \phantom{+}1 \end{array} \right] \hspace{10mm} U = \left[ \begin{array}{rrr} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & \phantom{+}1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]
\[\begin{split} \begin{align*} L_2 L_1 A &= U \\ A &= L_1^{-1} L_2^{-1} U \\ A &= LU \end{align*} \end{split}\]

where \(L = L_1^{-1} L_2^{-1}\).

Let’s verify the result:

L = np.array([[1,0,0],[1,1,0],[2,-1,1]])
print(L)

[[ 1  0  0]
 [ 1  1  0]
 [ 2 -1  1]]

U = np.array([[2,1,1],[0,-1,1],[0,0,3]])
print(U)

[[ 2  1  1]
 [ 0 -1  1]
 [ 0  0  3]]

L @ U

array([[2, 1, 1],
       [2, 0, 2],
       [4, 3, 4]])

Now compute with scipy.linalg.lu.

A = np.array([[2,1,1],[2,0,2],[4,3,4]])
p,l,u = la.lu(A)

print(p)

[[0. 0. 1.]
 [0. 1. 0.]
 [1. 0. 0.]]

print(l)

[[1.         0.         0.        ]
 [0.5        1.         0.        ]
 [0.5        0.33333333 1.        ]]

print(u)

[[ 4.   3.   4. ]
 [ 0.  -1.5  0. ]
 [ 0.   0.  -1. ]]

p@l@u

array([[2., 1., 1.],
       [2., 0., 2.],
       [4., 3., 4.]])

With partial pivoting the matrix \(L\) always has entries with absolute value less than or equal to 1 in the lower left. That is, \(|\ell_{i,j}| \leq 1\) for \(i > j\) where \(\ell_{i,j}\) is the entry of \(L\) in row \(i\) and column \(j\).

Example 2¶

The \(N\) by \(N\) matrix

\[\begin{split} A = \left[ \begin{array}{cccccccc} 2 & -1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 & & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & & \vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots & \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & -1 & 2 \\ \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]

is symmetric positive definite. Compute the solution of the system \(A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) for

\[\begin{split} \mathbf{b} = \left[ \begin{array}{r} 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]

by computing the Cholesky decomposition and using the function scipy.linalg.solve_triangular.

N = 20
A1 = 2*np.eye(N)
A2 = np.diag(-np.ones(N-1),1)
A = A1 + A2 + A2.T

plt.imshow(A,cmap='winter')
plt.colorbar()
plt.show()

Check the documentation for scipy.linalg.cholesky. The function returns an upper triangular matrix \(U\).

U = la.cholesky(A)

plt.imshow(U,cmap='winter')
plt.colorbar()
plt.show()

Solve the system:

b = np.ones([N,1])
y = la.solve_triangular(U.T,b,lower=True)
x = la.solve_triangular(U,y,lower=False)
plt.plot(x,'b.')
plt.show()
